Energy Level Diagram

As you draw your molecule, Orbis will display an energy level diagram in the lower left hand pane. Each line represents a molecular orbital energy level. If an energy level is degenerate than the lines for each of the degenerate orbitals will be shown side by side. An example of this (the energy level diagram of benzene) is shown below.

The color and line type of the energy level lines is used to indicate the number of electrons occupying that orbital (the number of electrons can be controlled using the spinner in the top left panel). In cases where the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) is degenerate the number of electrons in each degenerate orbital is calculated as Ne/N where Ne is the number of electrons available to fill the degenerate orbitals and N is the level of degeneracy. For example if there are 3 electrons to fill 2 degenerate orbitals, each orbital will be considered to be filled with 1.5 electrons.

The meaning of the different line types are as follows:

  • A solid black line indicates no electrons (Ne = 0)
  • A dashed blue line indicates the number of electrons is between 0 and 1 (0 < Ne < 1)
  • A solid blue line indicates the number of electrons is exactly 1 (Ne = 1)
  • A dashed red line indicates the number of electrons is between 1 and 2 (1 < Ne < 2)
  • A solid red line indicates the number of electrons is exactly 2 (Ne = 2)

The energy level diagram can also be used to control the molecular orbital plot panel (discussed below). Clicking on one of the energy level lines will cause the molecular orbital plot panel to display the orbital associated with that energy level (eigenvalue). The corresponding eigenvalue/eigenvector in the Eigenvectors results tab will also be highlighted. The currently selected molecular orbital is indicated in the energy level diagram by two circular symbols on either end of the line.

Results notebook

The tabbed notebook in the bottom right hand panel presents the following list of calculated parameters:

  • Eigenvalues and eigenvectors
  • Pi bond orders
  • Charge densities
  • Atom-atom polarizability
  • Atom-bond polarizability
